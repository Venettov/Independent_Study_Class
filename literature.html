Evidence from Demography, Economy, and Place

This page synthesizes research linking population decline, the island’s economic structure, poverty/housing conditions, and strategy options. Key claims are annotated with superscripts that map to Selected Sources below.

Population Dynamics¹

Low fertility + aging + out‑migration reinforce decline; the working‑age share shrinks, pressuring services and growth.

Economic Structure²

Post‑936 industrial shifts, exposure to shocks, and sectoral concentration limit wage growth and local multipliers.

Poverty & Housing³

Persistent poverty, housing vulnerability, and uneven recovery shape labor supply, migration decisions, and place quality.

Population (Signals & Mechanisms)¹

Population decline emerges from low TFR, aging, and net out‑migration. Shocks (e.g., 2017 hurricanes) amplify moves by younger cohorts seeking wages and stability. The feedback loop—smaller labor force → weaker demand → fewer local opportunities—can entrench decline.

¹ See sources 1–3.

Economic Structure (Constraints)²

Legacy of tax‑incentivized manufacturing and its phase‑out reduced anchors; limited tradable diversification, high logistics costs, and capital flight challenge productivity. Strategic bets should expand tradable services and value‑added niches that leverage talent and diaspora ties.

² See sources 4–6.

Poverty & Housing (Household Constraints)³

High poverty and housing fragility affect labor mobility and health. Recovery dollars improved stock unevenly; permitting, insurance, and hazard exposure remain frictions. Better targeting and resilient infill can reduce displacement and commute burdens.

³ See sources 7–9.

Strategy Lens (What Works)

Combine Talent (skills, retention, return migration), Firms (tradables, finance, permitting), and Places (housing, transport, resilience) to shift expectations. Pilot in a few metros; measure spillovers and iterate.
