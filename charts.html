Population & Economic Dynamics in Puerto Rico — Literature Review

A concise synthesis of peer-reviewed research, federal datasets, and policy analyses on demographic decline, economic structure, housing pressures, and strategic development pathways.

Demographic headwinds

Very low fertility, rapid aging, and sustained out-migration reinforce one another.

Economic fragility

Post-936 industrial decline, lower wages, and a shrinking labor base depress growth.

Household stress

High poverty and cost burdens intersect with environmental risk in housing.

Population Dynamics

Research identifies **migration as the dominant driver of rapid aging**, amplified after major shocks. A **very low total fertility rate** and a rising median age reduce future labor supply and increase dependency ratios.

  • Post-Maria migration spikes shifted age structure
  • Working-age outflows reduce human capital locally
  • Enrollment declines cascade into school closures
  • Aging raises health and social-service demand

Economic Structure & Performance

The **phase-out of Section 936** eroded manufacturing anchors and exposed structural weaknesses. Wages trail the U.S. average, and a shrinking workforce constrains consumption and tax revenues.

  • Lower average wages limit household demand
  • Employment losses concentrated in tradables
  • Consumer spending contracted in the 2010s
  • Small-firm formation & innovation are pivotal

Poverty, Inequality & Housing

**Poverty remains pervasive** across municipios, with high child poverty and pronounced inequality. Housing affordability is stressed and intersected by **hazard exposure** in vulnerable areas.

  • Cost-burdened households limit mobility
  • Median incomes remain low relative to costs
  • Exposure to landslide-prone terrain elevates risk
  • Targeted rehabilitation & resilience are key

Strategy Lens

A durable path centers on **locally anchored sectors**, talent retention, and neighborhood-level inclusion. Evidence points to pairing industrial strategy with human-capital and housing resilience.

  1. Rebuild tradables: biomanufacturing adjacencies, tech-enabled services, and visitor economy with local capture.
  2. Invest in skills: workforce pipelines tied to employer demand and returning migrants.
  3. Stabilize families: affordable housing, childcare access, and school modernization in shrinking-enrollment zones.
  4. Plan for risk: resilience upgrades in high-susceptibility areas; target buy-downs and relocation where needed.

What this means for policy & practice

Talent

Retain and attract working-age residents via career ladders and re-entry pathways for returnees.

Firms

Support clusters with supplier development, export help, and access to growth capital.

Places

Target neighborhood investments where poverty, school decline, and hazard exposure overlap.
